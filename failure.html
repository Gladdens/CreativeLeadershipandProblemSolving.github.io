Encouraging Failure

Introduction

There's a misconception that not reaching a goal initially equals failure (Thompson, 2021). Mistakes and adjustments are likely, especially when we're new to something (Thompson, 2021). As McGrath (1999) defines it, failure can simply be when an initial goal needs to be revised. This "failing forward" might be due to a shift in interests or long-term viability, leading us to set new goals that better suit the situation. However, societal views often make us see "losing" as shameful (McGrath, 1999), which can hinder this valuable learning process.

How Do We Encourage Failures?

  • You can learn what does not work, and use that as a strategy to learn from your failures, instead of moping around over it (Mcgrath, 2011)
  • It can build a culture that celebrates failure (Mcgath, 2011). Failure can be seen as a learning experience, so if people celebrate the “failures” instead of making fun of them, the success of the company will be higher.
  • Sharing what you learn with others is a great way to celebrate failures, as this allows you to feel comfortable within the community, and reflect upon that failure with other people (Mcgrath, 2011).
  • Keep your options open. “You’ll improve your odds if you make more tries” (Mcgrath, 2011).




How does a Failure Tolerant Leader look like in the workplace?

  • They get involved in projects by showing interest in them and asking questions about the progress (Farson & Keyes, 2002).
  • Admitting mistakes, if you make any rather than ignoring them or blaming them on someone else (Farson & Keyes, 2002) This allows for empathy to be given from the people that are working there.
  • Encourage everyone to share ideas with each other and learn from them (Farson & Keyes, 2002).
  • Listening to each other and adding any thoughts that they think are relevant to the conversation (Farson & Keyes, 2002).
  • Mistakes are okay to make, and are with it because they teach us important lessons (Farson & Keyes, 2002).




Embracing Failures

Encountering challenges and not immediately succeeding is a universal experience. When we face such moments, we commonly fall into self-doubt, pondering, "What's wrong with me?" Yet, it's essential to grasp that failing to accomplish a specific task doesn't equate to being a failure. Making this distinction is pivotal for our growth and continued efforts.
Countless individuals celebrated for their achievements have navigated through hardships. Their attitude towards these obstacles made all the difference. Take Erma Bombeck, for instance, a writer known for her wit, who faced significant health challenges. She chose not to view herself as a failure but instead shared her journey with humor, illustrating the importance of embracing both the ups and downs. She highlighted the crucial difference between identifying as a failure and experiencing failure in certain endeavors. This mindset is key.
This concept is far from novel. Renowned figures like Mozart, Van Gogh, Edison, and Einstein didn't always receive acknowledgment for their extraordinary talents. They encountered criticism and setbacks but refused to be defined by these moments. They persisted, learned, and progressed, exemplifying the essence of perseverance.

Reflect on Rudy's ambition to play football for Notre Dame. Despite not fitting the traditional mold of an athlete and facing academic challenges, his unwavering resolve propelled him toward realizing his ambition, reinforcing that determination can turn dreams into realities, despite hurdles.
Ultimately, viewing setbacks as opportunities for learning propels us forward, eliminating self-doubt. This mindset fosters resilience and paves the way for achieving our aspirations.

Conclusion

Although failure is viewed as a negative experience and often described as shameful, it is important that those experiencing failure understand the positive outcomes that come with it. Being able to use failure as a learning experience can enhance quality of problem solving later in life. The understanding of what works and what does not becomes more clear and the act of sharing or celebrating these failures can bring communities closer ultimately increasing connectivity and empathy with others. Failures should be embraced as it is an excellent opportunity to use the experience as an advantage should difficulties arise again.

Irani (2023) remarked, "Your failures define your path forward, and your successes mark the culmination of your efforts. Therefore, it is wise to treat success and failure alike,"
